FAA Airspace Waiver Helper

Completing Your Airspace Waiver Application

Use this guide to understand what to enter in each field of the FAA airspace waiver form.

UAS Operators Part 107
Tip: Keep this open while you fill out your application.
1
Waiver Dates & Timeframe
Define when your waiver starts, ends, and the hours you’ll operate.

Enter the first day you need the waiver authority to be active.

  • Use the actual calendar date.
  • For recurring events, choose the earliest day you might operate.

Enter the final day you need the waiver to remain active.

  • For ongoing commercial use, many operators select 1–2 years out (FAA allows up to 48 months for some waivers).
  • For a single event, use the event’s final day.

Use the drop-down menu on the form and select one or all that apply:

  • Sunrise to Noon
  • Noon to 4pm
  • 4pm to Sunset
  • Nighttime

Use the frequency drop-down and choose what best matches your operation.

Example: If you have two events within a year in Class B airspace, selecting “Monthly” may make the most sense.

  • Daily
  • Weekly
  • Bi-Weekly
  • Monthly
2
Location & Airspace Details
Describe exactly where you’ll fly and what airspace you’re in.

Identify the official name or description of the operating area.

  • Name of venue, park, or event site
  • Include nearest street address or known landmark
  • Include Latitude/Longitude

Example:
“Charlotte Motor Speedway, Concord, NC — NASCAR Race"

Enter the highest altitude you intend to fly, measured in feet Above Ground Level (AGL), not MSL.

  • Be realistic and prepared to justify the need in your CONOPS.
  • Requests must be at or below 400 ft AGL.
  • Review the FAA sectional chart grid where you’ll be flying to see if it has an altitude limit for automatic approval. If your grid has a defined altitude, you may be able to use LAANC. If your grid is 0’, continue with this application.

Provide the center point of your intended operations.

  • Use decimal degrees or degrees/minutes/seconds exactly as required by the form.
  • Verify coordinates using FAA UAS Facility Maps or tools like Google Earth.

Enter the distance from the center point where operations will occur.

  • Measured in feet or nautical miles, depending on the form.
  • Typical range: 100–1,500 ft for event venues; larger if surveying.

List the closest airport, heliport, or airfield.

Include the 3- or 4-letter identifier (ICAO/IATA), which you can look up here:

FAA Airport Identifiers Lookup

Indicate the controlled airspace classification at your location:

  • Class B
  • Class C
  • Class D
  • Class E Surface
  • Class G (if requesting a special waiver unrelated to controlled airspace)

Use FAA sectional charts or UAS Facility Maps to confirm the correct airspace class.

3
Operational Description & Safety
Explain what you’re doing and how you’ll keep it safe.

Give a concise operational overview. Briefly cover:

  • What you will do
  • Why you need the waiver
  • Type of aircraft
  • Operational environment

Example:
“Small UAS operations for aerial filming during nationally televised motorsports events. FPV drone and standard camera drone operations over portions of the venue with transient crowds. Waiver requested to safely conduct operations over people and in controlled airspace.”

If you or your organization already holds an FAA waiver, list it here:

  • Waiver number
  • Regulation waived
  • Expiration date

If none, simply enter: “None.”

This is often a more detailed and safety-focused expansion of your earlier summary. Write it like a mini CONOPS and cover:

  1. Purpose of operation
  2. Aircraft details (make/model, safety features, weight)
  3. Operational area & environment
  4. Crew roles (Pilot, Visual Observer, Safety Officer, etc.)
  5. Mitigation measures (cones, barriers, controlled access, geofencing)
  6. Risk controls (weather minima, lost-link procedures, emergency actions)
  7. Safety systems (Remote ID, ADS-B receivers, drone detection, kill switch, parachutes, etc., if applicable)

Focus on how you will identify hazards and what specific steps you will take to keep non-participants and other air traffic safe.

Use this guide as a reference only. Always follow current FAA guidance and instructions on the official waiver application form.
