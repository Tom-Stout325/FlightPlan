FAA Air Space Summary

This is a summary of the information required for your FAA airspace waiver request. When you begin a new AirSpace Waiver Request, AirSpace will walk you through all of the information your need.

Use this as a checklist while gathering information before starting a new waiver request. AirSpace will organize these details into a complete package for your FAA application.

Overall Operation Overview

High-level description of what you want to do under the waiver.

  • Purpose of the operation (e.g., live broadcast, event coverage, inspections).
  • General description of flight activities (shots, patterns, altitudes, proximity to people/structures).
  • Dates, times of day, and frequency of operations.
  • Type of environment (stadium, race track, urban downtown, rural site, etc.).
Operation Area & Airspace

Where the operation will take place and which airspace it affects.

  • Precise location (address, venue name, city/state).
  • Latitude/longitude for key points (launch site, flight box, recovery areas).
  • Maximum altitude AGL and terrain/elevation considerations.
  • Airspace class (B/C/D/E/G) and nearby airports/heliports.
  • Controlled-access areas vs. open public spaces.
Aircraft & Safety Features

The small UAS you will use and how it is configured for safe operations.

  • Drone make/model(s) and weight.
  • Safety features (obstacle sensing, RTH, ADS-B In, geo-fencing, prop guards if used).
  • Typical flight time, range, max speed used during operations.
  • Payload details (camera, lens, mounting, any external loads).
  • Fail-safe and loss-link behavior (RTH, controlled descent, termination plan).
Personnel, Roles & Experience

Who is responsible for the operation and how the crew is structured.

  • Remote Pilot in Command (RPIC) name & Part 107 certificate number.
  • Pilot flight experience (total hours, event-type experience, night/OOP experience).
  • Visual observers and other crew roles (camera op, safety officer, spotters).
  • Training and currency requirements for all crew members.
  • Responsible Party contact details for the waiver.
People, Vehicles & Ground Environment

Who is under/near the operation and how they are protected.

  • Types of people involved (participants, staff, spectators, public).
  • Presence of moving vehicles (race cars, support vehicles, public roads).
  • How access to the area is controlled (tickets, fencing, security, cones).
  • Standoff distances from crowds, grandstands, and vehicles.
  • Emergency access routes for first responders.
Risk Assessment & Mitigation Strategies

The hazards you’ve identified and how you will reduce risk to people and other aircraft.

  • Key hazards (collisions, loss of control, flyaway, system failures, weather, obstacles).
  • Mitigations for each hazard (geo-fencing, standoff buffers, VO coverage, speed/altitude limits).
  • Pre-flight and post-flight inspections and checklists.
  • Lost link, low battery, and emergency landing procedures.
  • Coordination with ATC or event control, if applicable.
Containment & Emergency Procedures

How you keep the drone and its risk inside a defined, safe area.

  • Defined horizontal “flight box” and vertical limits.
  • Use of software limits (altitude, distance, geo-fence) to enforce containment.
  • Designated emergency landing zones and safe corridors.
  • Actions on loss of GNSS, compass errors, or unexpected behavior.
  • Incident reporting and post-incident review process.
Communications, Weather & Operating Conditions

How the crew talks, monitors conditions, and decides if it is safe to fly.

  • Crew communication methods (radios, intercom, hand signals).
  • Standard callouts for low battery, link issues, people entering the area, and abort.
  • Weather minimums (visibility, winds, ceiling, no precipitation/smoke).
  • Night or low-light procedures (anti-collision lighting, night training, extra standoff).
  • Decision criteria for delaying, modifying, or canceling flights.
Documentation, Records & Compliance

What you will track and keep available to support the waiver.

  • List of approved aircraft and serial numbers used under the waiver.
  • List of qualified pilots, VOs, and their training/experience.
  • Flight logs for each mission (time, location, altitudes, anomalies).
  • Accident/incident logs and corrective actions.
  • Copies of the waiver, CONOPS, and any supporting safety documentation.
